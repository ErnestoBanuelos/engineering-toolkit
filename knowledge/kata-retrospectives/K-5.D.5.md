# K-5.D.5 Retrospective

## Kata

**K 5.D.5 — Produce a Brownfield Delta with honest REMOVED accounting.**

## Summary

This kata required producing a formal Brownfield Delta for a post-approval
change to an already-reviewed and validated feature specification. The scenario
was a Product Owner requesting a new classification tier (CRITICAL) after the
feature specification had been signed off at Reviewed status.

The kata produced a complete Brownfield Delta containing: a preserved behaviour
statement, five additions, six modifications, five removals, a REMOVED Audit
that identified one additional removal during the audit pass, a Risk Note
targeting the boundary between the new and preserved classification paths, a
Proof Test targeting that boundary, and a full Engineering Review.

The kata validated that the Brownfield Delta methodology surfaces consumer
impact that would not be visible in a standard specification amendment, and
that the REMOVED section is the discipline that most distinguishes rigorous
change governance from informal change description.

## Engineering Decisions Made During This Kata

### ED-1 — The Preserved Behaviour section must contain exactly one sentence

**Decision:** The Preserved Behaviour section of a Brownfield Delta must
contain exactly one sentence. It states the most important caller-visible
guarantee that the change must not break.

**Rationale:** During the kata, early drafts of the preserved behaviour section
listed multiple items. The discipline of reducing to one sentence forced a
genuine prioritisation: which invariant, if broken, would cause the most harm?
Multiple preserved behaviours dilute the risk signal; a single sentence
focuses it.

**Generalised as:** STD-006 Section 2.1, Rule PB-1.

---

### ED-2 — A REMOVED entry is required for every implicit consumer assumption, not only explicit guarantees

**Decision:** REMOVED must capture both explicit guarantees (documented in the
specification) and implicit guarantees (consumer assumptions reasonably
inferred from the specification as approved).

**Rationale:** During the kata's REMOVED Audit, the most valuable removals
were implicit: the guarantee that HIGH was the severity ceiling, the
assumption that a three-level parser was sufficient, the unconditional
five-block escalation cap. None of these were explicitly documented in the
specification as guarantees, but all were assumptions a reasonable consumer
would have made. Limiting REMOVED to explicit documentation would have missed
the most consumer-impactful items.

**Generalised as:** STD-006 Section 5.1 (Definition of removed behaviour),
Section 5.2 (Consumer assumptions category), and PB-002 Section 3 Technique 5
(Consumer implementation thought experiment).

---

### ED-3 — The REMOVED Audit must be procedural, not declarative

**Decision:** The REMOVED Audit is a structured procedure — applied item by
item to ADDED, MODIFIED, and each removal category — not a declaration that
"REMOVED is complete."

**Rationale:** Early draft REMOVED Audit sections in the kata consisted of a
single statement: "REMOVED is complete." The kata revealed that genuine
completeness could only be claimed after applying a systematic procedure
to every ADDED item, every MODIFIED item, and each category of common
removals. The procedural approach found one additional removal (R-5) that the
initial REMOVED section had missed.

**Generalised as:** STD-006 Section 6 (REMOVED Audit Methodology), WF-004
Stage 4 (REMOVED Audit stage with explicit procedure).

---

### ED-4 — The proof test must target the boundary between new and preserved behaviour, not the new behaviour's happy path

**Decision:** The proof test in the Risk Note must exercise the exact
boundary condition where the preserved behaviour and the new behaviour are
most proximate. Testing the new behaviour's happy path is not a proof test
for the preserved behaviour.

**Rationale:** During the kata, the initial proof test candidate demonstrated
that CRITICAL was produced correctly. This only tested ADDED. The kata
identified that the genuine risk was at the boundary: the condition where the
HIGH path and the CRITICAL path diverge (PDB presence vs absence). A test
that covers this boundary — specifically a case where no PDB is present and
the result must be HIGH not CRITICAL — demonstrates that the preserved
behaviour survives. This is the `HIGH-preserved-on-no-PDB` test.

**Generalised as:** STD-006 Section 7.3 (Proof test requirements), PB-002
Section 5 (How to write meaningful risk notes).

---

### ED-5 — Conditional relaxations of unconditional constraints produce two delta entries: one MODIFIED, one REMOVED

**Decision:** When a constraint changes from unconditional to conditional,
the delta must contain both a MODIFIED entry (the new conditional behaviour)
and a REMOVED entry (the loss of the unconditional guarantee). A single
MODIFIED entry is insufficient.

**Rationale:** The kata's escalation cap change illustrated this: the cap
moved from "≤5 unconditionally" to "≤6 when CRITICAL; ≤5 otherwise." This
is a modification of the constraint (MODIFIED), but it also removes the
unconditional guarantee (REMOVED). A consumer who built logic on the
invariant "no report will ever have more than five escalation blocks" will
break. Only the REMOVED entry makes this visible.

**Generalised as:** STD-006 Section 4 Rule M-2 (MODIFIED vs REMOVED
distinction), Section 5.2 (Unconditional constraint relaxations), PB-002
Section 6 Mistake 6 (Conditional relaxation treated as a modification).

---

### ED-6 — The Engineering Review must demonstrate examination, not assertion

**Decision:** The Engineering Review section must describe what was examined
and what was found or excluded. An answer of "yes, complete" without
examination rationale fails the standard.

**Rationale:** During the kata, each Engineering Review question was answered
with a structured examination: what candidates were considered for inclusion,
which were included, which were excluded and why. This demonstrated that the
review was genuine. An Engineering Review that consists only of "yes, X is
complete" provides no evidence of rigour and cannot be challenged by an
approver.

**Generalised as:** STD-006 Section 8.2 and 8.3 (Engineering Review
expectations and rejection of incomplete reviews).

## Lessons Learned

### L-1 — The REMOVED section is the most important section and the most commonly incomplete

The ADDED and MODIFIED sections in most change requests are naturally
produced: engineers know what they are adding and what they are changing.
The REMOVED section requires active effort because it demands the author
take the consumer's perspective and identify what the consumer could
previously rely on that they can no longer rely on.

In the kata, the most valuable REMOVED items were implicit: not documented
in the specification as explicit guarantees, but inferable from the
specification's structure, examples, and constraints. These are the items
that consumers silently break on.

The toolkit response is to elevate REMOVED to a named discipline with a
mandatory audit procedure, rather than treating it as a section to fill
in after ADDED and MODIFIED are done.

---

### L-2 — Post-approval changes are governance events, not informal amendments

A specification that has been approved and may be governing a running
implementation is a live contract. Amending it casually — without identifying
what the amendment removes from that contract — is the engineering equivalent
of changing an API without versioning it.

The Brownfield Delta is the mechanism that makes the amendment visible as
a governed event. The delta does not prevent the change; it makes the change's
consequences explicit, reviewable, and traceable.

---

### L-3 — Specification stranding is a real risk

The kata revealed a specific failure mode: a value (CRITICAL) was present in
the specification's classification table but stranded — no acceptance criterion
triggered it, no output annotation was required for it, and the rest of the
specification operated on an implicit three-level model.

A stranded specification element is worse than an absent one: it creates the
appearance of coverage while providing none. The delta formalised CRITICAL
by propagating it through every section that had remained on the old model.

The general lesson: when a new classification value is added to a scheme,
every section that references that scheme must be audited for implicit
assumptions about the previous set of values.

---

### L-4 — The REMOVED Audit can discover items the initial REMOVED section missed

In the kata, the REMOVED Audit found R-5 (the guarantee that AC-2 was the
highest-severity acceptance criterion) which the initial REMOVED section had
not captured. This confirms that the audit is not a formality: it is the step
that catches the items that are missed when the author is thinking about the
change rather than the consumer.

The audit procedure works because it forces systematic application of
removal categories to items that were not initially written as removals.

---

### L-5 — AI self-review of a delta it has just produced is limited

An AI that has just produced a delta has the change deeply in context. It
will tend to evaluate the REMOVED section against the change it just made
rather than against the consumer's perspective. The REMOVED Audit techniques
in PB-002 were specifically designed to counteract this bias by forcing
enumeration of specific categories that the change-focused perspective
tends to miss.

Human independent review remains essential. The automation can produce a
first draft that is structurally complete and internally consistent, but
the completeness of REMOVED requires a human who can genuinely take the
consumer perspective.

## Patterns Discovered

### Pattern — REMOVED Before ADDED

**Name:** REMOVED Before ADDED

**Context:** Any change analysis to an approved specification.

**Problem:** Authors naturally start with ADDED (what is new) and MODIFIED
(what changes). REMOVED is filled in last, under time pressure, and tends
to contain only explicit removals. Implicit removals — which are often more
impactful — are missed.

**Solution:** After drafting ADDED and MODIFIED, and before writing REMOVED,
apply the hidden removal search techniques systematically. Do not declare
REMOVED complete until the REMOVED Audit procedure has been applied.

**Consequence:** REMOVED sections become more complete. The REMOVED Audit
occasionally extends REMOVED after the initial draft, which is the intended
outcome.

---

### Pattern — Boundary-Targeted Proof Test

**Name:** Boundary-Targeted Proof Test

**Context:** Any change that introduces a new value adjacent to, above, or
overlapping with an existing value in a classification scheme or enumeration.

**Problem:** The natural proof test for a new classification value demonstrates
that the new value is produced correctly. This tests the ADDED behaviour but
not the preserved behaviour. The regression risk is at the boundary between
old and new behaviour.

**Solution:** Define the proof test as an input that falls under the preserved
behaviour's coverage but is closest to the new behaviour's trigger condition.
The test passes if the preserved output is produced. The test fails if the
new output is produced instead.

**Consequence:** The proof test becomes a regression detector, not just a
feature verifier.

---

### Pattern — Implicit Enumeration Ceiling

**Name:** Implicit Enumeration Ceiling

**Context:** Any specification that defines a classification scheme with a
fixed set of values, where the change adds a new value above the current
maximum.

**Problem:** Specifications document what the enumeration contains, but rarely
document what it does not contain. A consumer can reasonably infer "the
maximum value is X" without that invariant being explicitly stated. When a
new maximum value is added, the consumer's parser or switch statement breaks
silently.

**Solution:** When a new maximum value is added to any enumeration, the
delta must include a REMOVED entry for the implicit guarantee that "the
previous maximum was the ceiling." This removal is always present when the
maximum changes; the question is only whether the author remembers to state it.

**Consequence:** Consumer-breaking removals that were previously invisible
become explicitly documented. Consumers can assess their own exposure rather
than discovering the break in production.

---

### Pattern — Two-Entry Conditional Relaxation

**Name:** Two-Entry Conditional Relaxation

**Context:** Any change that converts an unconditional constraint into a
conditional one.

**Problem:** Conditional relaxation is naturally classified as MODIFIED: the
constraint still exists, just with conditions. But the unconditional form
of the constraint is also a consumer guarantee, and it disappears. Classifying
the change as only MODIFIED misses the REMOVED entry.

**Solution:** Conditional relaxations always produce two delta entries. One
MODIFIED entry describes the new conditional constraint. One REMOVED entry
describes the loss of the unconditional guarantee.

**Consequence:** Consumers who relied on the unconditional form receive
explicit notification that their assumption is no longer valid.

## Future Improvements

### FI-1 — Delta diff automation

A script that reads an approved specification at version N and at version N+1
and automatically identifies structural changes (new sections, new table rows,
new classification values) would reduce the effort of Phase 1 and Phase 2 of
the AI bootstrap workflow. This would be implemented as an Automation asset
and would depend on the specification following STD-004 structure.

### FI-2 — Consumer registry

The hidden removal techniques in PB-002 require knowledge of how consumers
are built. A consumer registry — a structured record of known consumers of a
capability's output contract — would make the consumer implementation thought
experiment more systematic. This would be a Context Bundle extension defined
in a future ADR amendment.

### FI-3 — REMOVED completeness heuristic

A heuristic that estimates whether a REMOVED section is plausibly complete
given the scope of the change (e.g., "a change that adds a new classification
value should have at least N removal entries covering enumeration ceiling,
consumer parser, and format stability") would help authors assess their own
REMOVED section before the audit. This would be added to PB-002 as an
additional section.

### FI-4 — Dual-reviewer delta protocol for high-risk changes

The current standard requires one independent reviewer. For high-risk changes
(those affecting capabilities with many known consumers, or those modifying
safety-relevant classification logic), a dual-reviewer protocol would
strengthen the evidence. This would be defined as a Level 4 governance
requirement in a future ADR amendment.

### FI-5 — Delta version convention

The relationship between specification versioning (1.0.0 → 1.1.0 for a
material amendment) and delta versioning (1.0.0 as the delta's own version)
is documented in practice but not yet in a formal standard. A versioning
convention that connects delta versions to specification versions should be
formalised in STD-006 or a companion document.
